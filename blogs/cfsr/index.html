An attempt to intuitively explain HDP, and why it makes sense to use HDPs for jointly doing mixture modeling of multiple related datasets. [wip]

Uber values economists a lot - their Ubernomics team is considered the most valuable of all. Economists love working with Uber for the huge amounts of data it provides, and also that they can apply their basic 101 theories and get actual results. This is very much unlike the rest of the world, where 101s are usually vague approximations. So, Uber gives the economists the data - and they conduct analysis of various things like job satisfaction of drivers, user satisfaction, comparison with other industries etc. What could possibly go wrong?

Company-funded research has been panned by many academicians for cheating with data and producing results in favour of the company. There is also the problem that companies are luring away well-respected academicians with money and data. For example, Alan Kruger - a well respected economist - conducted studies on Uber’s data. The study was overtly supportive of Uber - claiming that it increases satisfaction levels of both drivers and customers. The paper was panned by many academicians from prominent institutes like MIT, Stanford etc for wrong methodologies applied.

It’s not just Uber. Facebook’s head of Artificial Intelligence Research is Dr. Yann LeCun. LeCun is considered on of the most important personalities of the machine learning community, and the MNIST dataset he released is used by all ML aspiring students and researchers at some stage of their studies. He has come under criticism for Facebook’s wrong practices. Is it fair to say that we are losing great academicians to company-funded research field?

As a society, how much can we rely on their research now? If companies like Google, Facebook, Amazon bag up all the top academicians - can we still accept their research as honest and without any bias? It is important for governments to make interventions in these things and penalize the companies if they produce fake research. The issue is, maybe we all as a collective are not talented enough to catch the mistakes in their research in the first place. We still have a long way to go to cope up with these tech giants...
